Having read the novels, I thought that Yamada-san did a great job of adapting Ayano Takeda's 1st volume of the novel in a 13 episode format, which I found more stimulating than the anime when it came to the Kumiko/Reina interactions. She never really strayed from the same path of those scenes. There were some slight changes to the execution like bringing instruments to the mountain but overall, not too much was changed.

The circles I hung around with both in high school and college consisted of a few females who were considered "touchy-feely." But they were boy-crazy in both secondary school as well as university level.

Now those same female friends are middle aged and married women with families but just as touchy-feely as before. Some people are just like that and there are groups of reserved people who just aren't used to that. I live in Los Angeles so it's a pretty mixed bag of chips here and most people I know are extroverts including myself LOL. And I grew up in the 70s and 80s during my youth and I was surrounded by people who (in this day and age) would be considered stepping over bounderies. So straight women don't always abide by the same criteria you described.

In an interview that I posted a few months ago, Novel author, Ayano Takeda had mentioned that Reina was a sensual kind of person. You also have to keep in mind that she is someone who doesn't know social etiquete quite well seeing as she's been pretty much a loner. So her social graces are a bit on the extraordinary side. When you read the novel, you'll pick up on that.

Awhile ago @karice67 and @BBOvenGuy had pointed out the differences between "Ai" and Koi". Reina herself used "Ai". If you have the novel, check out page 198, bottom left of page. Reina uses "ai" (Kanji is 愛). Same confession as the anime.

The differences between the 2 words that have to do with love can be summed up as:

Ai (愛)= An unconditional kind of love that is considered a "giving" kind of love. This is the sacrificial kind of love that puts other people's needs before oneself. This is the genuine form of love.

Koi(恋) = a selfish, passionate love that is a "wanting" kind of love. The kind of love that wants to fulfill ones own desires while "ai" is the opposite. This is considered the "romantic love"


Regardless, I do enjoy the idea of a Kumiko/Reina pairing but to make this the front and center of the story is doing the entire series injustice and makes the series seem rather shallow. And the story is anything BUT. I thought the novels were beautifully done and Takeda did a great job of working with so many characters and having Kumiko interwoven into many of the arcs without stealing anyone's thunder as everything in the novel was from Kumiko's perspective.

Really, the story Hibike! Euphonium is a journey for Kumiko's self-discovery as she slowly but surely crosses that threshold of cynicism and learns to fully appreciate music. I really wish the novels were translated for the rest of you because I believe most or some would really appreciate and pick up on that nostalgia of youth. While watching the anime series, I felt the same kind of nostalgic feeling I did when I read the novels.

But to be fair, And I know I mentioned this in a post awhile ago but the Mangaka for Eupho, who happens to be Hami, had read the original novels and ALSO thought it was a yuri novel LOL

There is a term called "The Death of The Author"; it means the author's original intentions can be dismissed. For the sake of our argument, what the author have initially planed may change because, while working on the story, they discover their original plan no longer fit the direction the story took, or that the characters developed differently.

For example, let's say character Alice was supposed to kill character Bob, but as the author was writing, Alice's character developed and the action no longer made sense for her character. The author, in this case, has two options:
1. Going with their initial plan despite the whiplash it may produce.
2. Changing the plot to fit the contemporary personality of the character.

For a real-life example; the creators of The Legend of Korra hadn't planed for Asami and Korra to be a couple, but as characters changed on their own, it just seemed natural. So they changed the story.

What I'm trying to say is basically that Kumiko and Reina are obviously attracted to each other, and the writer is a moron to ignore that.

I thought I should clarify why I brought it up again. From an older discussion that I had with Kamieichi about the fanbook interview:

Quote:
Originally Posted by karice67
I was just wondering, could you tell me what word for ‘love’/romance Takeda, Ishihara and Yamada contrast 「友情」 with in that section of the interview?
Quote:
Originally Posted by Kamieichi
"恋愛"

Quote:
武田 （笑）。小説にかぎらずあらゆる創作物において、恋愛が友情の上に来ることが多いじゃないですか。それに納 得がいかなくて、この作品は友情が上でもいいんじゃないかと思って書いたんです。
Takeda: (laughs) In almost every work, not just novels, there's so many that put romance above fellowship, aren't there? I wasn't satisfied with that, so I wrote this work thinking of making fellowship above romance.
"Ren'ai" (恋愛) is a compound word that's composed of the kanji for "koi" and "ai." As you might guess, it refers to the 'koi' kind of love, and is usually used by creators to refer to the love that people tend to feel when they first 'fall in love' with someone, i.e. how people feel in the early stages of a relationship. I've seen the "ren'ai/koi" vs. "ai" difference used to distinguish between 'immature' and 'mature' feelings of love between couples. Another way I like to think about it is that, "ren'ai" is associated with 'blind love', love that blinds you to the other's flaws. In contrast, "ai" is associated with the love that accepts the other for who they are, perhaps even appreciating it.

And the 'love' that Takeda was placing below 'friendship' is, quite specifically, that 'immature'/'blind' one of "ren'ai."

Trumpets - Full of themselves because trumpet ego
